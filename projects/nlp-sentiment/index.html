NLP: Sentiment Analysis of Earnings Call Transcripts
NATURAL LANGUAGE PROCESSING  ·  SENTIMENT ANALYSIS  ·  FINANCE

Abstract

This project explores the dynamic shifts in sentiment expressed by CEOs and CFOs during economic crises and assesses the accuracy of their communications in such times. We analyzed earnings call transcripts from four distinct companies — Royal Caribbean (RCL), Norwegian Cruise Line (NCLH), Delta Air Lines (DAL), and Southwest Airlines (LUV) — applying the Loughran-McDonald sentiment analysis to texts from both executives. The sentiment scores obtained were regressed against share price data to gauge the correlation with actual company performance, with a particular focus on the COVID-19 crisis period.

2. Sentiment Analysis of Earnings Calls

The core analytical pipeline focused on extracting and quantifying sentiment from CEO and CFO speech sections within each earnings call transcript, using the Loughran-McDonald (LM) financial lexicon — a domain-specific dictionary designed for finance and accounting text where common words carry different sentiment than in general usage.

Pipeline Contents

  • Preprocessing: Lowercasing, stemming, removal of HTML tags, stopwords, and extra whitespace
  • Sentiment Scoring: Loughran-McDonald Sentiment Analyzer applied separately to CEO and CFO speech
  • Temporal Analysis: Sentiment scores plotted against share prices across Pre-COVID, COVID Lockdown, and Post-COVID periods
  • Regression Analysis: OLS regression to explore relationships between executive sentiment and market data

Libraries Used

  • yfinance — share price retrieval
  • Seaborn & Matplotlib — visualisation
  • Scikit-learn — regression analysis
  • BeautifulSoup & Selenium — data collection

Highlights

  • Preprocessing pipeline tailored for financial transcripts
  • Aggregated word cloud generation using TF-IDF weighting
  • Detailed sentiment analysis with focus on the COVID-19 economic downturn
  • Visualisation of CEO and CFO sentiment trends overlaid with stock prices
  • Regression analysis quantifying the relationship between sentiment and share performance

4. Conclusion

Our study revealed that CEOs typically showed higher sentiment scores than their CFO counterparts, but this gap narrowed markedly during the COVID-19 crisis, suggesting that extraordinary economic pressure compresses the divergence in executive communication styles. Notably, CFOs consistently provided a more accurate reflection of company performance relative to share prices across all four companies, suggesting they may speak with greater financial candor during earnings calls. These findings highlight the value of disaggregating executive sentiment in NLP-based financial analysis rather than treating management communication as a monolithic signal.

5. Further Study

Several avenues remain open for future research. First, incorporating time lags into sentiment scores would allow a deeper analysis of how executive communication influences future share prices rather than contemporaneous moves. Second, Vector Autoregressive (VAR) models could capture richer time-dependent interactions between CEO and CFO sentiment and market variables. Third, refining sentiment score computation by normalizing for speech length and speaking style — and applying TF-IDF weighting at the word level — would yield a more nuanced and equitable comparison across executives and companies.
